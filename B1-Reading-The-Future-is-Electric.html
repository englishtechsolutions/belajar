Reading Passage

Electric Cars in Indonesia: Sales Grow, But Still Small

Sales of electric cars in Indonesia grew 171% in the first half of 2025 compared to last year. But the total share is still small—only 5% of all cars sold. In Norway, electric cars already make up 90% of new sales because of strong public education and promotion.

Why People Still Hesitate

Many Indonesians are not ready to buy electric cars. They think the cars are expensive and not practical. Sales often depend on government subsidies and discounts. Chinese brands lead the market because they sell cars with modern features at lower prices.

Charging Problems

By 2024, Indonesia had only 3,356 charging stations. In China, there are 1 million. Another problem: each brand uses different charging sockets, unlike petrol cars where all tanks are the same.

Promotion Needs to Change

Car brands and the government should not only say electric cars are “green.” They should also show that they are:

  • Cheaper to run than petrol cars
  • Modern and stylish with smart apps and navigation
  • Part of a healthy, active lifestyle

Government Support & The Big Question

The government gives Rp6.16 trillion in incentives in 2025, including tax cuts. Chinese brands like Wuling, BYD, and Neta are already building factories in Indonesia. Hyundai also produces electric cars locally. Indonesia has 40% of the world’s nickel, a key material for batteries. But so far, the country is still more of a market and supplier than a global car producer. The future depends on how fast the government and industry can work together to build a strong local ecosystem.

True or False?

Read the sentences below. Are they true (T) or false (F) according to the article?

1. Electric car sales in Indonesia are decreasing.

Explanation: False. The article's first sentence says sales "grew 171%".

2. Most new cars sold in Norway are electric.

Explanation: True. The article states that in Norway, "electric cars already make up 90% of new sales".

3. The main reason Indonesians don't buy electric cars is the lack of stylish models.

Explanation: False. The article says people hesitate because the cars are "expensive and not practical", not because of style.

4. Indonesia has more charging stations than China.

Explanation: False. Indonesia has "only 3,356 charging stations", while "In China, there are 1 million".

5. All electric cars use the same type of charging socket.

Explanation: False. The text mentions that "each brand uses different charging sockets".

6. The Indonesian government is providing financial incentives to support electric car sales.

Explanation: True. The 'Government Support' section states, "The government gives Rp6.16 trillion in incentives... including tax cuts".

Discussion & Critical Thinking

Discuss these questions in detail with your teacher.

  • The article states sales often depend on subsidies and discounts. Do you think this is a good long-term strategy? What could happen if the government suddenly stops giving this financial help?
  • Why is having different charging sockets a big problem for consumers? What do you think the solution could be?
  • The text suggests promoting electric cars as "modern and stylish." Do you think this is a more effective marketing strategy than promoting them as "green" in Indonesia? Why?
  • Indonesia is a major producer of nickel but not cars. What advice would you give the government to build a strong local "ecosystem" for car manufacturing?
  • If you had to buy a new car tomorrow, would you choose a petrol car, a hybrid, or a fully electric car? Explain your decision based on your personal needs and the information in the article.
  • Prediction: The article is set in 2025. What do you predict the situation for electric cars in Indonesia will be in 2035? Will they be common or still rare? Justify your prediction.
  • Government's Role: Besides giving money (incentives), what are three other things the government could do to encourage people to switch to electric cars? (e.g., new laws, building projects, education campaigns).
  • City Life: How might a city like Jakarta change if 50% of the cars were electric? Think about noise, air quality, and the types of businesses (like petrol stations).

Scenario-Based Tasks: Choose One!

Choose one of the following scenarios to discuss with your teacher.

Scenario A: The Advertising Challenge!

  • Your Role: You are a marketing director for a new electric car brand in Indonesia.
  • Your Challenge: Create an advertising campaign to sell more electric cars. Your plan must directly address the problems of price and charging anxiety.
  • Your Plan: Explain your target audience, main message, and campaign idea. How will you make people feel confident about buying an electric car despite the challenges?

Scenario B: The Town Hall Debate!

  • Your Role: You are a citizen speaking at a town hall meeting.
  • The Debate Topic: "To fight pollution, the government should ban the sale of all new petrol cars in major cities starting in 2035."
  • Your Task: Choose a side—either you support this ban or you oppose it. Prepare three strong arguments to convince the audience. Use facts from the article and your own ideas to support your position.
