Part 1: Warm-up Discussion (Speaking)

  • The hashtag #KaburAjaDulu means "just escape first." What kind of problems in their home country might make young people want to "escape"?
  • What are the usual benefits people expect from working abroad?
  • Have you ever thought about working or studying in another country? Why or why not?
  • What comes to mind when you think of Australia?
  • Do you follow any influencers on social media who live abroad? What kind of life do they show?

Part 3: Reading Text

Read the following article. Don't worry about unknown words for now. Try to understand the main topic and the author's general message.

Australia: A Popular Place for Young Indonesians to Work and Travel

In early 2025, the hashtag #KaburAjaDulu became popular among young Indonesians. It means “just escape first.” Many young people use this idea to go to Australia with a Working Holiday Visa (WHV). They hope to earn money, enjoy beautiful views, and live a better life.

Why Australia?

Australia is a favorite because:

  • Jobs are simple, like gardening or serving food.
  • The pay is high—up to Rp300,000 per hour.
  • The country is beautiful and close to Indonesia.
  • Influencers show only the good parts of life in Australia.

Australia also needs workers, especially in farms and small towns. Local people don’t want to work in these areas, so the government welcomes foreign workers. The WHV program started in 1975 as a way for young people to learn about other cultures. Now, it helps Australia’s economy too.

Indonesians aged 18–30 can apply for WHV. They can work and travel for 1–2 years. In 2024, WHV workers helped Australia’s farming industry earn AU$3 billion (Rp32.3 trillion). If fewer people join, the country could lose AU$200 million (Rp2.1 trillion) each year.

What Do Indonesians Need?

To apply for WHV, Indonesians must:

  • Be 18–30 years old.
  • Take an English test (IELTS, TOEFL, or PTE).
  • Get a support letter (SDU-WHV) from the Indonesian government.
  • Have at least AU$5,000 (Rp54 million) in savings for 3 months.

The support letter is very important. Without it, the visa application will not be accepted. But the number of letters is limited—only 5,000 for the 2025/2026 program. This makes the process competitive, like buying concert tickets.

The Risks

Many people think WHV guarantees a job, but that’s not true. There is no official job placement. Most people find work through friends or social media. Without enough information, they may end up in hard jobs with low pay in remote areas.

Some workers face serious problems:

  • Scams and unpaid wages.
  • Dangerous jobs and accidents.
  • No legal protection or support.

In October 2024, two Indonesians died in a traffic accident in Western Australia. This showed the need for better safety rules and support for WHV workers.

What Should the Government Do?

The Indonesian government should help by:

  • Giving clear information about WHV.
  • Teaching workers about their rights.
  • Preparing them for cultural differences.
  • Protecting them from scams and unsafe jobs.

The #KaburAjaDulu trend shows how young people use the internet to share ideas and build global identities. The government should join these online spaces to give correct information and protect its citizens.

Part 8: Analyzing Perspectives (Discussion)

Discuss these "why" questions with your teacher.

  • For WHV Applicants: Why do you think so many young people are willing to take these risks?
  • For Australia: Why doesn't the Australian government give jobs directly to WHV holders?
  • For the Indonesian Government: The article lists four things the government should do. In your opinion, which one is the most important? Why?
  • For Influencers: Do you think social media influencers have a responsibility to show the "bad parts" of living abroad, not just the good parts?
  • For "Online Spaces": The article says the government should join online spaces. Do you think this is a good idea? How could they do it effectively?

Part 9: Scenario Task (Speaking)

Choose one or two scenarios to discuss and role-play with your teacher.

Scenario 1: The Optimist

Your 22-year-old cousin is very excited about the #KaburAjaDulu trend. He has saved AU$5,000 and wants to apply for the WHV immediately. He says, "I'll be rich in one year! It's going to be so easy, just like on Instagram." He asks for your opinion. Based on the article, what advice would you give him?

Scenario 2: The Pessimist

Your friend applied for the WHV, but their application was rejected. They didn't get the competitive support letter. They are very depressed and say, "My dream is over. I'll be stuck here forever." What advice or comfort can you offer them? (Think about other options or future plans).

Scenario 3: The Emergency

Your friend is already in Australia. They send you a message: "Help! My boss hasn't paid me for two weeks, and I work on a remote farm. I'm scared and don't know what to do. I don't have much money left." What practical steps would you advise them to take?

Part 10: Wrap-up & Reflection (Speaking)

Discuss these final questions with your teacher.

  • After reading and discussing this article, has your opinion about working abroad changed?
  • What is the most important lesson someone should learn before deciding to work in another country?
  • This article focuses on Indonesians. Do you think young people from other countries (e.g., Japan, Germany, Brazil) face the same problems with the WHV?
  • What new English word or phrase did you learn today that you want to remember?
