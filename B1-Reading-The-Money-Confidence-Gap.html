The Money Confidence Gap

Many people earn the same salary, but their feelings about money are very different. Some feel safe, save money, and plan for the future. Others worry about bills and fear they will never retire. This difference is called the money confidence gap. It is not about how much you earn, but about how confident you feel with money.

Why Confidence Matters

A study by Octopus Money showed that people from less wealthy families are less confident about retirement. Only 35% of working-class professionals believe they will have enough money to retire. In richer groups, 67% feel confident.

The same pattern happens with daily life. Just 35% of working-class people say their salary covers their needs. Among wealthier people, 67% say the same.

When it comes to investing, the gap is even bigger. Only 28% of people from poorer backgrounds invest for the future, compared to 63% of richer people. Even with the same salary, poorer workers are three times more likely to say they cannot pay an unexpected £500 bill. This shows that confidence, not just income, shapes how people manage money.

Real-Life Example

Ella Rathiel, a 26-year-old admin worker, grew up in a single-parent home. Money was never discussed, and saving was not part of life. For her, money was only about surviving.

At work, she joined a financial coaching session. She learned how to build an emergency fund and check her pension. After the session, she felt emotional but stronger. Debt was no longer a secret shame. She realised she was not alone, and money became more manageable.

How to Build Confidence

Experts say that financial education is the key. People who learn about saving, investing, and pensions feel more secure. Coaching and support can make a big difference.

Workers from less wealthy backgrounds who received coaching were 1.5 times more likely to feel confident about retirement. They were also 22% more likely to say they were financially resilient.

Five Simple Steps

  • Have a plan: Set short and long-term goals.
  • Build an emergency fund: Save 3–6 months of essential costs.
  • Start small: Even £50 a month can grow over time.
  • Know your outgoings: Track spending and find where to save.
  • Talk about money: Sharing ideas builds confidence.

Conclusion

Financial literacy is the missing link in social mobility. Without it, people may never feel safe enough to plan, save, or invest. As one expert said, “Social mobility without financial security is like climbing a ladder with missing rungs. You might start climbing, but you won’t get far.”

8 Scenario - Identifying the Mindset

Read the two profiles below. Based on the text's description of the "Money Confidence Gap," decide which person is suffering from low money confidence and explain why.

Profile A: David

David earns a high salary. However, he keeps all his money in a basic cash account because he is afraid of the stock market. He never looks at his pension paperwork because he thinks, "I'll never have enough anyway." When his friends talk about saving, he changes the subject.

Profile B: Maria

Maria earns the same salary as David. She saves a small amount (£50) every month into an investment fund. She knows exactly how much her electricity bill costs. She talks openly with her partner about their vacation budget.

1. Who has the "Money Confidence Gap"?

2. Give two reasons based on the text (e.g., investing, talking about money, fear).

Model Answers:
• Fear of investing/stock market.
• Avoids talking about money.
• Negative mindset ("never have enough").
