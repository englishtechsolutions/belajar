3. Reading Text

Redenomination in Indonesia: A Chance to Clean Up Dirty Money

Published: November 13, 2025

The idea of redenomination is back in the news. Bank Indonesia, the country’s central bank, supports this plan again after the draft law on changing the value of the rupiah was added to the national legislative program for 2025–2029.

Redenomination means reducing the number of zeros in the currency without changing its real value. For example, Rp1,000 could become Rp1. The goal is to make transactions easier and improve trust in the national monetary system.

But redenomination is not just about simplifying money. It can also help clean up illegal financial activities like corruption and money laundering. If done well, it can push people with large amounts of cash to report their assets honestly.

In Indonesia, cash still dominates 90% of all money used in daily economic activities. Many corruption cases also involve cash. For example, in one case, the Attorney General’s Office showed Rp2 trillion in cash from illegal palm oil exports. Criminals often use cash to avoid being tracked by digital systems like those used by the Corruption Eradication Commission (KPK) and the Financial Transaction Reports and Analysis Center (PPATK).

Redenomination can force people with large cash holdings to return to the formal financial system. This opens the door for better audits and transparency. It can also cause panic among those hiding illegal money, leading them to quickly move or exchange their assets — a behavior known as “panic laundering.”

Other countries have tried redenomination with mixed results. Turkey removed six zeros from its currency in 2005. At first, people were worried, but the government acted fast by improving bank audits and creating a national digital payment system. Two years later, Turkey’s corruption perception score improved.

On the other hand, Zimbabwe failed in 2008 when it removed 25 zeros without proper financial reforms. People lost their savings, and trust in the government collapsed.

To avoid failure, Indonesia must prepare well. There are three common signs of suspicious financial behavior:

  • Large cash deposits into inactive bank accounts.
  • Breaking big transactions into many small ones.
  • Quickly buying physical assets like gold, property, or luxury cars.

Indonesia already has digital financial systems like SPAN (State Treasury System), SIPD (Regional Government Information System), and e-tax. But the problem is not technology — it’s poor coordination between institutions. Data sharing is still slow and not real-time.

Redenomination can help improve national budget data and financial integrity. The government must prepare risk maps, strengthen anti-money laundering systems, and ensure open public reporting during the transition.

Clear communication is key. People must understand that redenomination does not reduce the value of their money. It only changes how it looks. Countries like Turkey and Ghana faced panic because of poor information, not the policy itself.

With strong planning and teamwork, redenomination can be a powerful tool to fight corruption and build a cleaner financial future for Indonesia.

8. Scenario-Based Tasks

Let's practice using these ideas. Choose one of the scenarios below to discuss or role-play.

Your Role: You

Partner's Role: Your elderly grandparent, who does not use digital banking and keeps a lot of cash saved at home.

The Situation: Your grandparent heard a rumor that the government is "deleting zeros" and is in a total panic. They think their life savings will become worthless.

Your Task:

  • Calm your grandparent down.
  • Explain simply what redenomination is (and what it isn't).
  • Reassure them that the value of their money is not changing. Use a simple example (e.g., "A bowl of bakso that costs Rp20,000 will now cost Rp20. Your Rp100,000 note will become a Rp100 note. It's the same thing.")
  • Explain why this is a good thing (e.g., "it's simpler," "it helps fight corruption").

Your Role: A bank manager.

Partner's Role: A small business owner (e.g., a cafe or warung owner) who has come to the bank for advice.

The Situation: The business owner is not worried about losing money, but is annoyed and confused about the practical side.

Partner's Lines: "This is going to be a disaster for my shop! Do I have to change all the prices on my menu? My customers will be confused! What about my old cash register? This is just too much work."

Your Task:

  • Show empathy ("I understand your concern...").
  • Explain the long-term benefits for them (simpler accounting, easier transactions).
  • Explain how the transition will work (e.g., "There will be a long transition period where both old and new money can be used...").

Your Role: A citizen at a community meeting (rapat RT/RW).

Partner's Role: An official from Bank Indonesia who is there to answer questions.

The Situation: You are at a public meeting about the redenomination plan. The official has just finished a presentation. It is now time for questions.

Your Task:

You have a strong opinion. First, choose your character:

  • Character 1: The Tech-Savvy Student. You support the plan. You think it's modern and will be great for the digital economy.
  • Character 2: The Skeptic. You are worried. You read about Zimbabwe. You don't trust the government to coordinate properly.

Based on your chosen character, ask the official (your partner) 2-3 difficult questions or make a strong statement about your opinion. Try to use vocabulary from the article (e.g., "transparency," "reform," "audit," "coordination").
