4. Analysis & Quiz

Part A: Comprehension Questions

1: David's first compliment is that he calls the plan "ambitious."

2: David's two main concerns are about the short timeline and the large scope (getting buy-in from three departments).

3: Anna says that she has already had informal chats with the other departments.

4: Overall, David's feedback is constructive but shows he has serious concerns. He praises the core idea ("solid concept," "excellent goal") but questions if the plan is feasible right now.

5. Language Practice: Giving Constructive Feedback

The following statements are too direct. Rewrite them to be more constructive and polite, using the vocabulary from this lesson.

1. Direct: "This is a bad idea."

Suggestion: "I have a few reservations I'd like to discuss about this idea."

2. Direct: "I don't like the design."

Suggestion: "The overall structure is solid. I just have a small concern about the color scheme."

3. Direct: "Your plan is impossible and won't work."

Suggestion: "This is a very ambitious plan. I'm not sure if it's fully feasible in its current form."

4. Direct: "You need to finish this by tomorrow."

Suggestion: "Is it realistic to expect this to be finished by tomorrow? That sounds like a bit of a stretch."

5. Direct: "Everyone will hate this new rule."

Suggestion: "I'm concerned that it will be difficult to get buy-in from the team on this new rule."

6. Discussion: Communication and Culture

Discuss these questions in a small group.

  • In the dialogue, David uses indirect language. What effect does this have on his professional relationship with Anna? What might happen if he was very direct?
  • Cultural Context: In many professional settings, especially in high-context cultures like Indonesia, maintaining harmony (kerukunan) is important. Indirect communication helps to do this by allowing people to disagree without causing offense. How does this compare to communication styles you are familiar with?
  • When is it absolutely necessary to be direct at work, even if it might feel less polite?

7. Role-Play: Workplace Scenarios

Read the following scenarios. For each one, write down what you would say in the given situation. Try to use today's vocabulary and strategies to reach a positive outcome.

Scenario A: Changing the Work Schedule

Your Manager's Idea: To change the team's working hours from 9:00 AM - 5:00 PM to 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM to better serve international clients.

Your Situation: You are concerned this change will cause problems for you with childcare and transportation. You need to express your reservations constructively and suggest an alternative (e.g., a flexible schedule).

Suggestion: "Thanks for sharing this idea. I can see how the new schedule would help with our international clients. I do have a small concern about how the 10-6 schedule would work with my childcare arrangements. Would it be feasible to consider a more flexible schedule? Perhaps some of us could adopt the new hours while others maintain the original schedule?"

Scenario B: Feedback on a Report

Your Colleague's Work: They have written a very long and detailed 20-page report. They are proud of it and ask you for quick feedback before sending it to your very busy boss.

Your Task: The information is solid, but you know your boss will only read a 2-page summary. You need to give feedback suggesting a major change without discouraging your colleague.

Suggestion: "Wow, this is really thorough. The concept is solid and you've clearly put a lot of work into the details. I have one suggestion: since the boss is so busy, do you think it might be a good idea to create a short, two-page executive summary to put at the front? That way, she can get the main points quickly and then dive into your detailed analysis."
